About Me

I'm a Chicago-based developer working in React, Ruby/Rails, JavaScript, VueJS, PostgreSQL, and HTML/CSS/Bootstrap. My professional experience includes proposal development, research, writing, and international teaching and volunteering.

I tracked myself into the humanities at a tender age, and viewed tech as something of a taboo. “We don’t go over there. That’s where the Math People live.” Instead, I became a writer, and spent several years trying to succeed in a field where the really plum opportunities start with “PAID:” I had a lot of adventures - a year in Cambodia, two in Argentina, two in Wyoming - which you can read about below. The photos on this site were taken in Cambodia.

One evening, I went to happy hour with a developer friend. They told me about a problem they were grappling with: employees at a client company couldn't save multiple search results with the same name without breaking the program. I was listening to all this and I thought to myself, “Wait, this isn’t math! This is about language! I know how to use language! I can do software! I can have an interesting career in a high-demand field where you get to solve problems and learn things!”

Then I enrolled in a bootcamp and now I am a developer.

Why I Code


"All my wire sculptures start from the same loop."

- Ruth Asawa

Ruth Asawa was a Japanese-American artist who became famous for her biomorphic wire sculptures. (You can see some examples here.) She studied and taught at Black Mountain College, an interdisciplinary art school. A core principle of her institution was that traditional crafting techniques - weaving, for example - were as impressive as fine art painting and sculpture. At a time when consumer culture was trending towards the slickly mass-produced, she and her contemporaries revived an artisanal approach. The work she created was valuable and beautiful in part because her careful work was visible.

When I viewed computer technology from a distance, I interacted with it at a superficial level. My career prospects were impoverished as a result. Learning to view coding as a set of creative techniques has changed my relationship with the world. I don't feel disadvantaged. I feel curious. I want to build applications that show users where they are in the technologies they use. I want to make this creative work visible to them, so that they can engage with it as deeply and personally as any work of art.

Boilerplate App

Inspired by my prior experience as a proposal writer, Boilerplate is a grantwriting tool designed to help nonprofits fundraise more effectively. Boilerplate allows users to create a library of text sections sorted by category, and then use that library to develop a grant in an intuitive online template.

I'm currently partnering with two nonprofits, MIRA and Scarleteen, to implement the application at their offices. The plan is that any staff member will be able to generate a grant application in a few hours. For this project, I'm using a Ruby/Rails backend, Vue frontend, and HTML/CSS/Bootstrap styling.
